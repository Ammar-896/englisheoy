Speckled Band Revision

The Speckled Band — Story Summary

Dr Watson recounts one of Sherlock Holmes' most dangerous cases. A frightened young woman, Helen Stoner, arrives at 221B Baker Street seeking help. She lives at Stoke Moran Manor with her violent stepfather, Dr Grimesby Roylott.

Two years earlier, her twin sister Julia died suddenly before her wedding. On the night of her death, Julia had heard a strange whistle for several nights. During a violent storm, Helen heard Julia scream and rushed to her. Julia stumbled into the corridor, crying out: "The speckled band!" — then died. The door was locked inside, windows barred, and no poison was found.

Now engaged, Helen has been forced into Julia's old room due to "repairs." She hears the same whistle. Holmes and Watson travel to Stoke Moran. Holmes notices a ventilator connecting Roylott's room to Julia's, a dummy bell-rope, and a bed fixed to the floor. He decides to spend the night in the room.

In the dark, they hear the whistle. Holmes sees a snake slithering down the bell-rope. He strikes it with his cane — the snake returns through the ventilator and bites Dr Roylott, killing him. Holmes reveals Roylott trained a poisonous swamp adder to kill his stepdaughters to keep their inheritance. "The speckled band" was the snake.

Characters

Sherlock Holmes

Point: Presented as intelligent, observant, calm under pressure.
Evidence: "You have come in by train this morning." — He deduces this from mud on her jacket and a train ticket.
Explore: Holmes notices details others miss, making readers trust his intellect. His calm contrasts with others' panic, showing intelligence overcomes evil.

Dr John Watson

Point: Loyal, brave, supportive sidekick and narrator.
Evidence: "Then I shall certainly come." — He agrees to danger without hesitation.
Explore: Watson's fear makes Holmes seem sharper. As narrator, he guides readers through the mystery step by step.

Helen Stoner

Point: Frightened, vulnerable, desperate for help.
Evidence: "It is fear, Mr Holmes. It is terror." — Repetition of "fear" and "terror" emphasises her state.
Explore: She feels trapped but is brave enough to travel to London alone. Conan Doyle creates sympathy for her plight.

Dr Grimesby Roylott

Point: Violent, intimidating, dangerous villain.
Evidence: "He hurled the local blacksmith off a bridge." — The verb "hurled" shows extreme strength.
Explore: Described as a "fierce old bird of prey." His exotic pets, dark appearance, and isolated home create a Gothic atmosphere.

Julia Stoner

Point: Terrified and helpless before her death.
Evidence: "Her face blanched with terror." — "Blanched" means pale, showing intense fear.
Explore: Her death is the central mystery. "The speckled band!" confuses readers and builds intrigue. Her experience helps Holmes solve the case.

Figurative Devices

Simile

What it is: Compares using "like" or "as".
Example: "swaying to and fro like that of a drunkard" — shows weakness and helplessness.
"his hand closed like a vice upon my wrist" — strength and sudden fear.

Metaphor

What it is: Direct comparison without "like" or "as".
Example: "These are very deep waters." — mystery is dangerous and hidden.
"gave him the look of a fierce old bird of prey" — Roylott as dangerous predator.

Personification

What it is: Human qualities to non-human things.
Example: "The wind was howling outside." — creates frightening atmosphere.
"the rusty iron gate creaked angrily" — hostile, unsettling setting.

Pathetic Fallacy

What it is: Weather reflects mood.
Example: "It was a wild night." — storm mirrors danger and chaos.

Foreshadowing

What it is: Hints of future events.
Example: Julia hearing the whistle before death — seems mysterious, later becomes key clue.

Imagery

What it is: Descriptive language that paints a picture.
Example: "her face all drawn and grey" — creates sympathy for Helen.

Symbolism

What it is: Object/phrase represents deeper meaning.
Example: "The speckled band" — symbolises the deadly snake and hidden danger.
